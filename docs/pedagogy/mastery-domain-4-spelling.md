## Domain position

Within the broader taxonomy:

- ultimate outcome:
  - communicate clearly and independently through meaningful work
- capability area:
  - writing
- mastery domain:
  - spelling and orthographic knowledge

Spelling and orthographic knowledge is the first fully instantiated mastery domain because:
- the repo already captures real writing evidence
- the parent-review workflow is already live
- the durable issue lifecycle already exists
- spelling is the first domain where reviewed issues already become canonical `learning_items`

## Domain boundary

Domain 4 covers the child's ability to spell words accurately by understanding how English spelling works.

It includes:
- hearing and segmenting sounds in spoken words
- mapping sounds to letters and graphemes
- choosing the correct grapheme for a sound
- applying common spelling patterns and conventions
- using syllable structure to support spelling
- using morphology, base words, suffixes, and word families
- handling unstressed vowels and schwa
- choosing homophones by meaning and context
- recognising etymological spelling patterns
- proofreading and correcting spelling in real writing

It does not primarily include:
- handwriting formation
- typing fluency
- general composition quality
- broad vocabulary development, except where word knowledge supports spelling
- punctuation, except where proofreading overlaps with written accuracy
- reading comprehension
- grammar, except where grammar affects spelling, such as contractions or homophones

## Why Domain 4 is cognitively multi-layered

Spelling is not one skill.

Accurate spelling requires the child to coordinate:

```text
spoken word awareness
-> sound awareness
-> phoneme segmentation
-> letter-sound knowledge
-> grapheme awareness
-> sequential mapping
-> grapheme choice
-> spelling patterns
-> morphology
-> word meaning
-> proofreading
-> transfer into real writing
```

A child may spell a word correctly in a drill but misspell it in a real story.
That means Domain 4 must track not only whether the child knows a spelling, but also whether they can apply it independently in authentic writing.

Domain 4 therefore needs three connected structures:

```text
1. Developmental foundation pathway
2. Strand / skill-family taxonomy
3. Micro-skill model with competency levels
```

## Domain 4 developmental foundations

These stages are not a replacement for skill families.
They help diagnose what kind of spelling thinking is involved.

| Stage | Foundation | Role in spelling development |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Oral word awareness | Child understands that spoken language is made of words |
| 2 | Syllable awareness | Child can hear and work with syllables in words |
| 3 | Rhyme and onset-rime awareness | Child notices rhyme, word endings, and initial sound chunks |
| 4 | Phoneme awareness | Child can hear individual sounds in spoken words |
| 5 | Phoneme segmentation | Child can split spoken words into individual sounds for spelling |
| 6 | Letter-sound knowledge | Child knows common links between letters and sounds |
| 7 | Grapheme awareness | Child understands that one sound can be represented by one or more letters |
| 8 | Sequential sound-to-spelling mapping | Child can map sounds to spellings in the correct order |
| 9 | Grapheme choice | Child can choose the correct spelling pattern for a sound |
| 10 | Orthographic pattern control | Child can apply English spelling conventions and visual patterns |
| 11 | Morphological spelling control | Child can use base words, suffixes, and meaning units to spell accurately |

Illustrative diagnostic examples:
- weak segmentation may show up as omitted sounds or compressed words
- weak sequential mapping may show up as `plae` for `play`
- weak grapheme choice may show up when `/ai/` is spelled with the wrong pattern for the context
- weak morphological control may show up when pronunciation is followed instead of base-word structure

These stages should guide interpretation where useful.
They should not replace the canonical family and micro-skill taxonomy.

MVP planning rule:

```text
Domain 4 developmental foundations are primarily pedagogical and diagnostic.
They may inform scaffolding and assignment planning.
They do not replace the taxonomy.
They are optional runtime tags in MVP, not required fields.
The MVP should not block on every learning item carrying a developmental foundation stage.
```

## Skill-cluster and micro-skill examples

Representative examples:

| Strand / cluster | Micro-skill example | Practice examples |
|---|---|---|
| Long /ai/ grapheme choice | Spell final /ai/ words with `ay` | day, play, say, tray, stay |
| Long /ai/ grapheme choice | Spell medial /ai/ words with `ai` | rain, train, paint, snail |
| Common pattern `ck` | Use `ck` after a short vowel at the end of a word | duck, back, sick |
| Morphology | Spell base + `-ment` words while preserving the base word | payment, enjoyment, agreement |
| Inflectional endings | Spell past tense `-ed` even when it sounds /t/ | jumped, looked, helped |
| Homophones | Choose `there`, `their`, and `they're` by sentence meaning | there, their, they're |

These are representative examples, not the final exhaustive curriculum.

## Competency levels in spelling

Competency levels describe how independently and reliably the child can use a linked spelling micro-skill.

They do not replace the taxonomy.
They sit alongside the micro-skill and help distinguish recognition, prompted correction, controlled practice, transfer, and retained mastery.

Example:

```text
Micro-skill:
Spell final /ai/ words with ay

Level 1:
Recognises final `ay` examples.

Level 2:
Corrects `plae` -> `play` with parent prompt.

Level 3:
Spells `day`, `play`, `tray`, `stay` correctly in a targeted lesson.

Level 4:
Uses final `ay` words correctly in real writing.

Level 5:
Retains and applies the pattern reliably over time in mixed writing and review.
```

This means spelling mastery should not be treated as a simple binary.

A child may therefore have:
- a current competency such as Level 2 or Level 3
- a target competency such as Level 4 or Level 5

The important distinction is:
- taxonomy describes what the skill is
- competency describes how well the child can use it

Minimum evidence principles:

| Level | Meaning | Minimum evidence principle |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Recognition / exposure | child recognises the skill or examples with support |
| 2 | Guided correction | child can correct or apply the skill with prompt/support |
| 3 | Independent controlled use | child can use the skill in targeted practice without help |
| 4 | Transfer to authentic work | child applies the skill in real writing/submission |
| 5 | Secure retained mastery | child applies the skill over time after delay/mixed review |

MVP planning rule:

```text
MVP should treat competency as per-child-per-micro-skill state supported by evidence history.
Exact thresholds should be conservative and domain-specific.
Level 4 requires authentic transfer.
Level 5 requires retained success across time.
```

## Route fit in Domain 4

Different spelling micro-skills need different practice routes.

Recommended route fit:
- single target word practice
  Best for isolated high-frequency irregular words.
- grouped set practice
  Best for a small cluster of near-related examples.
- sound or pattern practice
  Best for pattern-based families such as short vowel plus `ck`.
- morphology lesson
  Best for suffix changes and base-word preservation.
- dictation
  Best when the child needs to hold spelling while producing language.
- sentence application
  Best for contrast words and transfer into meaning-rich writing.
- proofreading
  Best when the issue is partly known but fragile under independent writing conditions.

The route should follow the micro-skill, not the other way around.

## Domain-specific evidence logic

Inside spelling, evidence should come from both:
- direct practice performance
- later transfer into fresh writing

Strong spelling evidence usually includes:
- accurate performance across more than one occasion
- retention across time
- correct use in sentence or project context
- reduced recurrence in authentic work

Weak spelling evidence usually includes:
- one immediate correction after being shown the answer
- one successful burst of repetition in the same sitting
- accuracy only in copied form

Positive evidence types for Domain 4:

| Evidence type | Meaning | Competency signal |
|---|---|---|
| `authentic_correct_use` | correct use in real submitted work | Level 4 evidence |
| `delayed_authentic_correct_use` | correct use in real work after time delay | Level 5 evidence candidate |
| `repeated_correct_use` | correct use across multiple later submissions | strong Level 5 evidence candidate |
| `parent_confirmation` | parent confirms it was genuine independent use | allows meaningful competency update |

Rules:
- one correct authentic occurrence should increase evidence rather than force mastery
- repeated correct use over time supports retained mastery
- parent confirmation remains required before competency changes materially

## Formal Level 4 and Level 5 scoring policy

The runtime should score higher spelling mastery as a per-child-per-micro-skill judgment.

This means:
- a level belongs to the child + micro-skill relationship
- words are evidence opportunities, not the mastery object by themselves
- harder vocabulary can strengthen or challenge a micro-skill only when it honestly tests that same micro-skill
- failure on a more complex or different word should not demote an unrelated mastered micro-skill

### Level 4 formal rule

Promote a micro-skill to Level 4 only when all are true:
- evidence comes from authentic writing, not controlled practice
- the child shows correct use in 5 distinct words
- those 5 words are matched through:
  - exact watched target words, or
  - curated related watch words for the same micro-skill
- all 5 words honestly map to the same canonical micro-skill
- there is no meaningful contradictory failure for that same micro-skill during the active Level 4 qualification window
- the parent confirms the evidence reflects genuine independent use
- later authentic success may still be confirmed as evidence while contradiction pauses Level 4 promotion

Interpretation:
- Level 4 means the child can use the micro-skill in real writing, not only in training
- one strong authentic success should add evidence, but should not force Level 4 by itself
- repeating the same exact word should not be enough to prove transfer

### Level 5 formal rule

Promote a micro-skill to Level 5 only when all are true:
- Level 4 has already been reached
- the child shows correct authentic use across 5 distinct later pieces of work
- each piece of work can count at most once toward retained-mastery evidence for that micro-skill
- the evidence shows at least 2 complexity bands where that micro-skill has genuine lexical range
- there is no meaningful contradictory failure for that same micro-skill during the active Level 5 qualification window
- the parent confirms the pattern looks secure and retained
- contradiction pauses Level 5 promotion, but does not invalidate a genuine later authentic success as evidence

Interpretation:
- Level 5 means the child keeps using the micro-skill successfully over time, after delay, in later authentic work
- Level 5 should not be awarded from one burst of success in a single submission
- Level 5 should not rely on one easy repeated word only

### Slippage and contradiction rule

For Levels 4 and 5, "no slippage" means:
- no meaningful contradictory failure in the same micro-skill during the relevant qualification window

Rules:
- contradiction is evaluated at the same micro-skill, not at the whole family or domain
- a harder or newer word only counts as contradiction if the parent can honestly say the failure primarily belongs to that same micro-skill
- unrelated vocabulary growth should create new evidence or a new issue, not automatically lower an older mastered micro-skill

Example:
- secure spelling of `duck`, `back`, and `sack` should not be lost just because the child later misspells `acknowledgement`
- that later error may reveal a different morphology or orthographic problem, but it is not automatically contradiction of the earlier `ck` pattern mastery

### Complexity policy for Level 5

The runtime should use a hybrid complexity source:
- primary source: curated seed data and word-bank difficulty
- fallback source: lexical or dictionary enrichment for unseen words

Normalize complexity into these internal bands:
- `basic`
- `extended`
- `complex_transfer`

Rules:
- complexity supports retained-mastery judgment, but does not replace taxonomy truth
- the taxonomy and parent confirmation remain primary
- if a micro-skill only has one practical band in MVP, require the maximum available spread rather than blocking Level 5 permanently
- absence of an error should never count as positive evidence by itself

### Positive evidence scope rule

Later authentic writing should only produce Level 4 / Level 5 candidates when the system finds a genuine opportunity to demonstrate the micro-skill.

For MVP, candidate positive evidence should come from:
- exact watched target words
- curated related watch words

It should not come from:
- broad open-ended pattern inference
- "no spelling error detected"
- dictionary complexity alone

## Fragility rule

A spelling skill is fragile when the child can sometimes perform it but cannot yet rely on it independently.

Typical signs:
- correct in practice but wrong again in writing
- correct with prompting but not alone
- unstable across near examples
- mixed success inside a contrast set

Fragility is pedagogically important because it often means:
- the child is not starting from zero
- but the skill should still remain active and reviewable

## Relationship to issue classification

Durable issue classifications remain separate from competency levels.

Use this distinction:

```text
Issue classification = what kind of problem this issue appears to be.
Competency level = how well the child can perform the linked micro-skill.
Lifecycle state = where the issue is in the workflow.
```

Examples:

```text
Issue:
plae -> play

Issue classification:
concept_gap

Linked micro-skill:
Spell final /ai/ words with ay

Competency level:
Level 1 or Level 2
```

```text
Issue:
Child spells `play` correctly in a lesson but writes `plae` in a story.

Issue classification:
transfer_failure

Linked micro-skill:
Spell final /ai/ words with ay

Competency level:
Level 3, not yet Level 4
```

```text
Issue:
Child corrects `plae` to `play` immediately after rereading.

Issue classification:
checking_only or not_an_issue

Linked micro-skill:
Spell final /ai/ words with ay

Competency level:
Potentially Level 4 or Level 5, depending on evidence history
```

## Cross-domain overlap

Some errors sit across domains.

For example:

```text
their / there / they're
```

can involve:
- Domain 4: spelling and homophones
- Domain 6: vocabulary and morphology
- Domain 7: grammar and sentence structure

Recommended default:
- one primary domain should anchor the issue or micro-skill
- secondary cross-domain links may exist where they help teaching or review
- Domain 4 remains the first deep implementation in the MVP

## Transfer rule

Spelling mastery is not complete just because isolated practice improved.

The system should still care whether the child can carry the spelling into:
- a new sentence
- a new task
- a later project submission

That is why real writing remains the top-layer evidence source.
